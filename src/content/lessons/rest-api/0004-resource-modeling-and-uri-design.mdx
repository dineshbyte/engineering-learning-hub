# —— interview Q&A (rendered by <Interview>; also the FAQPage JSON-LD source) ——
interview:
    - level: 'core'
      q: 'Why is <code>POST /getOrder</code> an anti-pattern?'
      a: >-
          <p>It encodes the <em>verb in the path</em>, which is RPC wearing a REST costume. In
          resource-oriented design the URI names a <strong>noun</strong> — the thing — and the HTTP
          method <em>is</em> the verb. The read should be <code>GET /orders/42</code>: the resource
          is <code>/orders/42</code>, the action is <code>GET</code>.</p>
    - level: 'core'
      q: 'What is the difference between a collection URI and an item URI?'
      a: >-
          <p>A <strong>collection URI</strong> (<code>/orders</code>) addresses the set:
          <code>GET</code> lists it, <code>POST</code> adds to it (server assigns the id). An
          <strong>item URI</strong> (<code>/orders/42</code>) addresses one member: <code>GET</code>
          reads it, <code>PUT</code>/<code>PATCH</code> updates it, <code>DELETE</code> removes it.
          The method's meaning shifts with which level you're at.</p>
    - level: 'core'
      q: 'What does nesting like <code>/customers/1/orders</code> communicate?'
      a: >-
          <p>Containment — "the orders belonging to customer 1." That's a legitimate, readable use of
          nesting for a real ownership relationship. The mistake isn't nesting one level; it's going
          deep.</p>
    - level: 'core'
      q: 'Why prefer opaque identifiers over sequential integers in URIs?'
      a: >-
          <p>Sequential ints <strong>leak business volume</strong> (reading order <code>#100002</code>
          tells an attacker your throughput) and invite <strong>enumeration</strong> — walking
          <code>/orders/1</code>, <code>/orders/2</code>… UUIDs/ULIDs are opaque and non-guessable, so
          neither attack works from the id alone.</p>
    - level: 'senior'
      q: 'Why does the noun/verb discipline matter beyond aesthetics?'
      a: >-
          <p>Because it's what keeps the contract <strong>uniform and cacheable</strong>. Once verbs
          leak into paths, every endpoint becomes a bespoke procedure and the HTTP machinery —
          caching, idempotency, conditional requests, status semantics — stops applying. <code>GET
          /orders/42</code> is cacheable and safe by definition; <code>POST /getOrder</code> tells
          intermediaries nothing. Naming the resource correctly is the load-bearing decision the rest
          of the design hangs off.</p>
    - level: 'senior'
      q: 'What are the three principled ways to model a non-CRUD action?'
      a: >-
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Sub-resource / state collection:</strong> treat the outcome as a thing —
            <code>POST /orders/42/cancellations</code>, or a status you read back.</li>
            <li><strong>Custom method (Google AIP):</strong> colon-suffixed verb on the resource —
            <code>POST /orders/42:cancel</code>.</li>
            <li><strong>Reify the action as its own resource:</strong> promote the verb to a noun —
            <code>POST /refunds</code> with <code>{order_id}</code>.</li>
          </ul><p>The senior signal is naming the trade-off, not just picking one.</p>
    - level: 'senior'
      q: 'Why is deep nesting like <code>/customers/1/orders/42/items/9</code> fragile?'
      a: >-
          <p>It hard-codes the entire hierarchy into the URL. The day a relationship changes — items
          move under a fulfillment, an order spans customers, you add line-item history — every client
          URL breaks. Deep paths couple your contract to today's org chart. They also bloat with
          redundant ids you don't need to locate the leaf.</p>
    - level: 'senior'
      q: 'What is a BOLA / IDOR vulnerability and how does id choice relate to it?'
      a: >-
          <p>Broken Object-Level Authorization (a.k.a. IDOR): a user swaps the id in
          <code>/orders/42</code> for <code>/orders/43</code> and reads someone else's data because
          the server checks <em>authentication</em> but not <em>ownership</em>. Opaque ids make
          resources <em>harder to guess</em>, but they are <strong>not the fix</strong> — they're
          defense in depth. The real fix is an authorization check on every object access ("does this
          caller own this order?"). Calling opaque ids a substitute for authz is a junior mistake.</p>
    - level: 'staff'
      q: 'Articulate the pure-REST vs pragmatist tension on custom methods.'
      a: >-
          <p>Purists argue <em>everything</em> should be a resource: a custom method like
          <code>:cancel</code> smuggles a verb back into the URI and breaks the uniform interface, so
          they'd reify state into resources (a cancellation record, a status sub-resource).
          Pragmatists accept a colon-method for a genuinely non-CRUD verb because inventing nouns
          nobody queries is its own cost. The staff answer doesn't pick a religion — it picks by
          lifecycle: durable state and history → reify; momentary transition → sub-resource or custom
          method. Say the trade-off out loud; that's the signal.</p>
    - level: 'staff'
      q: 'What are the downsides of a flexible <code>?expand=</code> / field-selection mechanism?'
      a: >-
          <p>It pushes you toward GraphQL-shaped problems without GraphQL's tooling: every expansion
          combination is a distinct query and cache key, so <strong>caching fragments</strong>;
          arbitrary expansion enables expensive N+1 / fan-out queries you must bound; authorization
          must be enforced <em>per expanded field</em>, not just on the root; and response shape
          becomes dynamic, complicating client typing and contract tests. Mitigate with an allowlist
          of expandable relations, depth/complexity limits, and per-field authz. At some point if
          clients need fully arbitrary shapes, that's the signal to consider GraphQL.</p>
    - level: 'staff'
      q: 'How does URI shape interact with object-level authorization?'
      a: >-
          <p>Identity-in-path makes the authorization target explicit: every <code>/orders/{id}</code>
          access needs an ownership check (BOLA defense), and consistent URI structure lets you
          enforce that with uniform middleware rather than ad-hoc checks. Aliases like
          <code>/users/me</code> remove a whole class of IDOR by not letting clients name other
          principals. Nesting can <em>imply</em> scope (<code>/customers/1/orders</code>) but you must
          still verify the caller may act as customer 1 — the path is a claim, not proof. Flat URIs +
          per-object authz is usually cleaner than relying on nesting for security.</p>
    - level: 'design'
      q: 'Model an e-commerce checkout (cart, place order, cancel, refund) on a whiteboard.'
      a: >-
          <p><b>A strong answer covers:</b> Cart is <code>/carts/{id}</code> with line items at
          <code>/carts/{id}/items/{id}</code> — one level, real containment. Place order: <code>POST
          /orders</code>, server assigns id, returns <code>201</code> + <code>Location</code>, built
          from the cart; require an <code>Idempotency-Key</code> so a retry doesn't double-charge.
          Cancel is a transition on an existing order, so <code>POST /orders/{id}/cancellations</code>
          or AIP-style <code>POST /orders/{id}:cancel</code> — no top-level noun. Refund has its own
          lifecycle (processed/settled/reversible) so <strong>reify</strong> it: <code>POST
          /refunds</code> with <code>{order_id}</code>, beside <code>/payments</code>. Ids opaque
          (UUID/ULID); list a customer's orders via <code>/orders?customer_id=</code> rather than deep
          nesting. That hits nouns-not-verbs, principled action modeling with a stated trade-off,
          shallow nesting + filtering, and security-aware ids.</p>
    - level: 'design'
      q: 'Design the resource model for a multi-tenant project-management API with users, projects, tasks, comments, and memberships.'
      a: >-
          <p><b>A strong answer covers:</b> Top-level collections by identity: <code>/projects</code>,
          <code>/tasks</code>, <code>/users</code> — each has its own life. Scope tenancy implicitly
          from the authenticated principal (resolve the tenant from the token), not via a
          <code>/tenants/{id}/…</code> prefix clients can tamper with. Tasks belong to a project, but
          keep nesting shallow: <code>/projects/{id}/tasks</code> for the owned list, and address a
          single task flatly by identity as <code>/tasks/{id}</code> so it doesn't break if it's
          re-homed; filter cross-cuts with <code>/tasks?assignee=…&amp;status=open</code>. Comments
          are owned by their parent and have no independent life, so <code>/tasks/{id}/comments</code>
          (one level). Membership is a many-to-many between users and projects that <em>carries
          data</em> (role, joined-at), so <strong>reify the join</strong>: <code>/memberships</code>
          (or <code>/projects/{id}/members</code> as a filtered view) rather than deep-nesting under
          one side. Ids opaque (UUID/ULID); enforce per-object authorization on every access (BOLA)
          <em>and</em> tenant isolation so user A can't read project B in another tenant — the path is
          a claim, not proof. Use <code>/users/me</code> for the caller. Conventions consistent
          throughout; <code>?expand=</code> bounded by an allowlist if clients need related data
          inline.</p>

# —— design-round rubric (rendered by <Interview> above the design questions) ——
rubricIntro: >-
    <b>A framework for whiteboarding a REST resource model — narrate each step out loud:</b>
rubric:
    - >-
        <strong>Clarify the domain &amp; list candidate nouns.</strong> Name the things the domain
        holds (order, cart, customer, payment, refund). Those are your candidate resources before
        any URL is drawn.
    - >-
        <strong>Top-level collections vs owned sub-resources, by identity.</strong> Ask "does this
        have identity on its own?" If yes it's a top-level collection; if it only exists inside a
        parent, it's an owned sub-resource (one level).
    - >-
        <strong>Map verbs to HTTP methods; place non-CRUD verbs deliberately.</strong> CRUD →
        standard methods. For genuine verbs (cancel, publish, refund) choose sub-resource, custom
        method (<code>:verb</code>), or reify-as-resource — driven by lifecycle, not grammar.
    - >-
        <strong>Relationships: one-level nesting for ownership, filtering otherwise.</strong> Nest
        <code>/customers/1/orders</code> only for true containment; express the rest with
        <code>/orders?customer_id=1</code>. Reify many-to-many joins that carry data.
    - >-
        <strong>Identifiers: opaque external ids + per-object authz.</strong> Use UUID/ULID, never
        the raw DB key. Opaque ids are defense in depth, not the authz fix — every object access
        still needs an ownership (BOLA) check.
    - >-
        <strong>Conventions &amp; consistency.</strong> Lowercase, hyphenated, plural collections,
        no extensions; query carries filter/sort/page, never identity. Predictability across the
        surface beats local cleverness.
    - >-
        <strong>Evolvability / versioning cost.</strong> Stable nouns, identity-in-path, and
        external ids let you add fields and expansions without breaking clients — name where each
        choice would force a version bump later.

## The trap: verbs in the path

<p>
    The fastest way to flag yourself as mid-level is to draw <code>/getOrder</code>,{' '}
    <code>/createOrder</code>, <code>/cancelOrder</code>. That's RPC in a REST costume: the verb is
    in the path and the HTTP method is now decoration. Name the <strong>noun</strong>; let the
    method be the verb acting on it.
    <sup><a href="#s1" id="r1">1</a></sup>
</p>

<Fig2 />

<div class="myth">
    <div class="m">
        ✗ Myth: "REST just means JSON over HTTP, so <code>POST /createOrder</code> is fine."
    </div>
    <div class="t">
        ✓ Truth: the URI names a noun; the <em>method</em> is the verb — that uniformity is what
        makes the contract cacheable and predictable.
    </div>
</div>

## Modeling actions that aren't CRUD

<p>
    Plain CRUD maps cleanly onto methods. It gets interesting at a genuine verb — <em>cancel</em>,{' '}
    <em>refund</em>, <em>publish</em>, <em>retry</em> — that doesn't reduce to
    create/read/update/delete. Three principled moves; the senior signal is naming the trade-off,
    not just picking one.
</p>

<div class="steps">
    <div class="step">
        <b>a · Sub-resource</b> Model the outcome as a thing:{' '}
        <code>POST /orders/42/cancellations</code>. Stays purely resource-oriented; the cancellation
        becomes a first-class record you can audit and list.
    </div>
    <div class="step">
        <b>b · Custom method (AIP)</b> A colon-suffixed verb on the resource:{' '}
        <code>POST /orders/42:cancel</code>. Honest that it's non-CRUD, keeps the resource in the
        path, explicitly sanctioned for verbs that don't fit.
        <sup><a href="#s1">1</a></sup>
    </div>
    <div class="step">
        <b>c · Reify as a resource</b> Promote the verb to a noun: <code>POST /refunds</code> with{' '}
        <code>{'{order_id}'}</code>. Best when the action has its own lifecycle, state, and history
        — it earns standalone existence.
    </div>
    <div class="step">
        <b>The tell either way</b> Whichever you pick, the resource still lives in the path and the
        method stays standard — you never invent a free-floating <code>/doThing</code>.
    </div>
</div>

<p class="pull">
    <strong>The trade-off to say out loud:</strong> purists reify state into resources; pragmatists
    allow a custom method for a genuinely non-CRUD verb. Pick by <em>lifecycle</em> — durable state
    and history → reify; a momentary transition on an existing resource → sub-resource or custom
    method beats inventing a noun nobody queries.
</p>

## Nesting: containment, but shallow

<p>
    Nesting communicates a real relationship: <code>/customers/1/orders</code> reads as "the orders
    belonging to customer 1." Legitimate. The mistake is going deep —{' '}
    <code>/customers/1/orders/42/items/9</code> hard-codes a whole hierarchy into the URL, and the
    day a relationship changes, every client breaks.
    <sup><a href="#s2" id="r2">2</a></sup>
</p>

<Fig3 />

## Identifiers, relationships, conventions

<p>Three more decisions an interviewer expects you to have opinions on.</p>

<div class="facts">
    <div class="fact">
        <b>Identifiers — prefer opaque</b> Sequential ints leak business volume (order #100002
        reveals throughput) and invite enumeration / BOLA. UUIDs or ULIDs are opaque and
        non-guessable. Slugs are for humans/SEO, never stable keys. (Deep dive in Lesson 8.)
    </div>
    <div class="fact">
        <b>Relationships — link, embed, or separate</b> Link via hypermedia (HATEOAS), embed/expand
        on demand (<code>?expand=customer</code> / <code>?fields=</code>), or expose separate
        endpoints. Over-embedding bloats payloads; under-embedding makes clients chatty with N+1
        round-trips.
    </div>
    <div class="fact blue">
        <b>Conventions — boring on purpose</b> Lowercase, hyphen-separated, no trailing slash, no
        file extensions. Query params carry filtering / sorting / pagination — never identity.
    </div>
    <div class="fact blue">
        <b>Singleton sub-resources</b> Not everything is a collection.{' '}
        <code>/users/me/settings</code> is a singleton — one per user, no id, no plural. Modeling it
        as <code>/users/me/settings/1</code> tells the interviewer you reached for the pattern
        reflexively.
    </div>
</div>

<div class="rule">
    <span>🧭 Memory rule:</span> URI names a <span>noun</span>, method is the verb; reify only when
    the action has its own lifecycle; nest ~1 level and filter for the rest; ids opaque; consistency
    across the whole surface beats local cleverness.
</div>
